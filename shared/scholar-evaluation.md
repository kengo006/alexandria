# Scholar evaluation

A repeatable rubric for evaluating **someone else's** scholarly work — a paper, proposal, review, or argument — when deciding whether to trust, cite, or adopt it. Complementary to the Critic: the Critic reviews *your own* drafts; this evaluates *external* work. Typically run by the Librarian on demand ("seriously vet this source before we rely on it"), especially for contested, small-press, or suspicious material.

## Scope first

Define the object (empirical paper / theoretical paper / literature review / proposal / chapter / essay), then the range: **comprehensive** (all dimensions), **targeted** (one or two), or **comparative** (several works on one rubric).

## Scale

Per applicable dimension, 1–5: **5** excellent, publishable · **4** good, minor revisions · **3** usable, real gaps · **2** weak, major revision · **1** poor, validity or clarity problems · **N/A** not applicable.

## Dimensions

1. **Question** — clear and specific? contribution meaningful? scope and assumptions explicit? question matches the claimed contribution?
2. **Literature & context** — relevant prior work covered? synthesis or bibliography-dumping? gaps identified accurately? recent and foundational work balanced?
3. **Method / approach** — can the method answer the question? choices defended? materials described clearly? could someone retrace the argument?
4. **Evidence / textual support** — sources credible and apt? coverage adequate? selections accounted for? bias risks discussed?
5. **Argument / analysis** — reasoning sound? comparisons fair? alternative explanations considered?
6. **Results & interpretation** — presented clearly? claims within the evidence? negative findings and counterexamples handled honestly?
7. **Limitations** — specific rather than boilerplate? speculation separated from the established?
8. **Writing & structure** — argument followable? organised around the question? terms defined? register precise?
9. **Citations** — do cited works actually support the claims they're attached to? primary sources where possible? reviews and preprints marked? citation metadata correct?

## Process

1. Read abstract / introduction / conclusion for the *claimed* contribution. 2. Read method and results for evidence quality. 3. **Take the strongest claims and check them against the works they cite** — this step catches more than everything else combined. 4. Score each applicable dimension. 5. Separate **fatal blockers** from **revision suggestions**. 6. Close with concrete next steps.

## Output

```markdown
# Scholar evaluation: <object>
## Verdict: score <1–5/NA> | confidence <high/med/low> | summary <3–5 sentences>
## Dimension scores
| Dimension | Score | Evidence | Revision priority |
## Fatal problems
## Suggested revisions
## Evidence still to verify
```

## Pitfalls

- Scores never replace concrete feedback.
- Don't penalise a work for a dimension outside its scope.
- **Never treat citation counts, journal prestige, or author fame as evidence of quality.**
- Don't accept a claim because it appears in the abstract.
